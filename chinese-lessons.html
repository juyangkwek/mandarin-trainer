PART 2对话Dialogues

The introduction — line by line

Turn both off to rehearse from characters alone.
01 · OPENING
大家好！很高兴认识大家。
Dàjiā hǎo! Hěn gāoxìng rènshi dàjiā.
Hello everyone! Very happy to meet you all.
Why this phrasing 大家好 is the universal, warm opener — safe for any mixed audience. A native executive at a formal external event might open with 各位领导、各位来宾 ("respected leaders, honoured guests"), but that register would sound rehearsed from a learner. If the room is very formal, add 各位好 (gèwèi hǎo) first — a nod to formality without overreaching.
02 · NAME & ROLE
我叫郭祖扬 — 祖先的祖，扬名海外的扬。我是丰益国际集团，专门负责糖业方面的负责人。
Wǒ jiào Guō Zǔyáng — zǔxiān de zǔ, yángmíng hǎiwài de yáng. Wǒ shì Fēngyì Guójì Jítuán, zhuānmén fùzé tángyè fāngmiàn de fùzérén.
My name is Guo Zuyang — the zǔ of "ancestors", the yáng of "famous overseas". At Wilmar International Group, I am the person specifically responsible for the sugar business.
Why this phrasing Always say 丰益国际 — Wilmar's Chinese name — never "Wilmar"; it signals respect and China-market familiarity. Queenie’s correction: a bare 负责人 is too vague — in Chinese usage it sounds like someone running an event, and listeners can’t place what you actually do ("a CEO is a CEO — he isn’t ‘in charge of’ anything"). Since Wilmar trades far more than sugar, 专门负责糖业方面 makes the scope explicit while keeping the humble register she agreed suits you. She also added the Chinese name custom: spell out your characters — 祖先的祖，扬名海外的扬 — so people retain your name, with a self-aware touch of humour ("famous overseas — why not?").
03 · TENURE
我在丰益工作了十六年。关于糖方面的业务，我已经做了十年。
Wǒ zài Fēngyì gōngzuò le shíliù nián. Guānyú táng fāngmiàn de yèwù, wǒ yǐjīng zuò le shí nián.
I have worked at Wilmar for sixteen years. As for the sugar side of the business, I have already been doing it for ten years.
Why this phrasing Tenure is currency in Chinese business culture — loyalty and long service confer credibility that titles alone don't. Sixteen years in a group founded by Kuok Khoon Hong says "trusted insider". Grammatically it's the simple 了 + duration pattern — natural for your level while doing serious credibility work. Queenie’s fixes: it’s 十六年, never 十六个年 — no measure word with 年 — and watch your y-/i- initials: 业 (yè) tends to get swallowed.
04 · BACKGROUND
以前，我负责集团的商业发展，比如并购和合资项目。
Yǐqián, wǒ fùzé jítuán de shāngyè fāzhǎn, bǐrú bìnggòu hé hézī xiàngmù.
Before that, I was responsible for the group's business development — for example M&A and joint venture projects.
Why this phrasing 并购 (M&A) and 合资 (JV) are above a rusty learner's level — and that's exactly why they're credible: professionals always know the vocabulary of their own work first. 合资 will also resonate — Wilmar's China business is historically built on JV structures, so the word carries weight with this audience. Queenie’s swap: 商业发展 (or 商务发展), not 业务发展 — on the mainland, 做业务 connotes sales, so the old phrasing risked casting you as a salesman. And it’s 合资项目 (projects), never 合资业务.
05 · LANGUAGE DISCLAIMER
我的中文不太好，请大家多多包涵。
Wǒ de Zhōngwén bú tài hǎo, qǐng dàjiā duōduō bāohan.
My Chinese is not very good — please bear with me.
Why this phrasing The line doing the most cultural work. Self-deprecation about ability — especially language — is good manners, and from a senior foreigner it's genuinely endearing. 多多包涵 is a set phrase every speaker recognises. Expect warm laughter and 「你的中文很好！」 — deflect with a smile and 「哪里哪里」 (nǎli nǎli). In class, Queenie endorsed this line exactly as written — and confirmed 包涵 over 指教, which would mean "please teach me."
06 · CLOSING
希望以后我们多多合作。谢谢大家！
Xīwàng yǐhòu wǒmen duōduō hézuò. Xièxie dàjiā!
I hope we can work together more in the future. Thank you all!
Why this phrasing Chinese introductions close forward-looking, oriented to the relationship, not the transaction. 多多合作 echoes the 多多 of the previous line — a rhetorical rhyme natives will feel. For an internal audience, swap in 请多多指教 ("please give me your guidance") — powerful from a newly promoted leader.

Tier 2 — the same introduction, native executive register

Why show you this: not to learn — to hear the gap. This is how a native Chinese executive of your seniority would say the same thing. Comparing the two makes the register choices in your version deliberate rather than accidental.
各位领导、各位嘉宾，大家好。我是丰益国际糖业及乙醇能源板块的集团负责人，兼任丰益糖业私人有限公司董事总经理。我加入丰益已有十六年，其中十年深耕糖业。此前，我负责集团层面的商业发展，主导过多个并购与合资项目。今后还请各位多多支持，期待与大家深化合作。谢谢！
Gèwèi lǐngdǎo, gèwèi jiābīn, dàjiā hǎo. Wǒ shì Fēngyì Guójì tángyè jí yǐchún néngyuán bǎnkuài de jítuán fùzérén, jiānrèn Fēngyì Tángyè Sīrén Yǒuxiàn Gōngsī dǒngshì zǒngjīnglǐ. Wǒ jiārù Fēngyì yǐ yǒu shíliù nián, qízhōng shí nián shēngēng tángyè. Cǐqián, wǒ fùzé jítuán céngmiàn de shāngyè fāzhǎn, zhǔdǎo guò duō gè bìnggòu yǔ hézī xiàngmù. Jīnhòu hái qǐng gèwèi duōduō zhīchí, qīdài yǔ dàjiā shēnhuà hézuò. Xièxie!
Respected leaders, honoured guests, hello everyone. I am the Group Head of the Sugar & Ethanol Energy segment at Wilmar International, and concurrently Managing Director of Wilmar Sugar Pte Ltd. I have been with Wilmar for sixteen years, ten of them deeply rooted in the sugar business. Prior to this, I was responsible for group-level business development, leading numerous M&A and joint venture projects. I ask for your continued support, and look forward to deepening our cooperation. Thank you!
What makes it native — five register markers 各位领导、各位嘉宾 — formal Chinese addresses the room in hierarchical order (leaders, then guests) before "everyone". 板块 ("segment") and 兼任 ("concurrently serve as") are corporate-register words that carry your full title structure. 深耕 — literally "deep ploughing" — is the executive's favourite metaphor for long expertise in one field; it compresses "10 years in sugar" into something with weight. 此前 / 主导 replace the everyday 以前 / 负责 — same meaning, higher register. And the close, 期待与大家深化合作, is aspirational and relationship-forward — the professional cousin of your 多多合作. Notice what didn't change: the humility. Even the native version asks for support (多多支持) rather than announcing authority.

The business — Tier 1, your deliverable version

Design logic: ten short sentences, ≈ 60–75 seconds, built almost entirely on two patterns you already own — 我们是最大的… ("we are the largest…") and 在 [place]，我们… ("in [place], we…"). The numbers are the hard part; everything else is repetition of a frame, which is exactly what makes it deliverable under pressure.
01 · THE MODEL
我们的糖业务是一体化的，我等一下会展开讲。
Wǒmen de táng yèwù shì yìtǐhuà de, wǒ děng yíxià huì zhǎnkāi jiǎng.
Our sugar business is integrated — I’ll expand on that in a moment.
Why this phrasing一体化 is above conversational level but it is the industry word for "integrated" — a domain word you're expected to own. Everything else in the sentence is elementary, which is the pattern throughout: simple frame, one professional term. Queenie’s addition: signpost it — 我等一下会展开讲 ("I’ll expand shortly") — so listeners outside sugar know the elaboration is coming instead of being left with a question.
02 · FOOTPRINT
我们在澳大利亚、新西兰、印尼、缅甸、印度和沙特都有自己的糖厂和品牌。
Wǒmen zài Àodàlìyà, Xīnxīlán, Yìnní, Miǎndiàn, Yìndù hé Shātè dōu yǒu zìjǐ de tángchǎng hé pǐnpái.
In Australia, New Zealand, Indonesia, Myanmar, India and Saudi Arabia, we have our own sugar mills and brands.
Why this phrasingCountry names are the memorisation load here — they're in the flashcard deck below. Note the shortened forms natives use in speech: 印尼 not 印度尼西亚, 沙特 not 沙特阿拉伯 — using the short forms is itself a fluency signal. Queenie’s upgrade: drop 实业 for the concrete 自己的糖厂和品牌 — her point was commercial as much as linguistic: owning mills sounds far bigger than "having business" ("buying and selling sugar, one office can do — a plant is different"), and brands make it tangible.
03 · THE CONNECTOR
通过贸易，我们把所有的业务连在一起。
Tōngguò màoyì, wǒmen bǎ suǒyǒu de yèwù lián zài yìqǐ.
Through trading, we join all the businesses together.
Why this phrasingThis uses the 把 construction — O-Level grammar that will resurface once you say it a few times. It's also the strategic heart of the whole introduction, so it gets its own short sentence and deserves a pause after it.
04 · SCALE — TRADING
我们是全球最大的糖贸易商，一年的贸易量达到一千二百万吨。
Wǒmen shì quánqiú zuì dà de táng màoyìshāng, yì nián de màoyìliàng dádào yìqiān èrbǎi wàn dūn.
We are the world's largest sugar trader — annual traded volume reaching twelve million tonnes.
Why this phrasingChinese counts large numbers in 万 (ten-thousands): 12 million = 一千二百万 ("one thousand two hundred ten-thousands"). This is the hardest cognitive switch for English-dominant speakers — practise the number as one memorised chunk, never as a live conversion. Plain 吨 replaces "MT", as a speaker would. Queenie added the frame: 一年的贸易量达到… ("annual traded volume reaches") — 贸易量 names the thing, 达到 gives the number a verb.
05 · TRADING NETWORK
我们的贸易办公室在新加坡、日内瓦、圣保罗和迪拜。
Wǒmen de màoyì bàngōngshì zài Xīnjiāpō, Rìnèiwǎ, Shèngbǎoluó hé Díbài.
Our trading offices are in Singapore, Geneva, São Paulo and Dubai.
06 · MARKET POSITION
在巴西、泰国和澳大利亚，我们的贸易份额都是第一。
Zài Bāxī, Tàiguó hé Àodàlìyà, wǒmen de màoyì fèn'é dōu shì dì yī.
In Brazil, Thailand and Australia, our trade share is number one.
Why this phrasing — a deliberate simplificationThe percentages (30/40/50%) are dropped in this tier. Rattling off 百分之三十、百分之四十、百分之五十 mid-speech is a genuine stumble risk, and 都是第一 ("all number one") lands the point harder anyway. Keep the percentages in reserve: if asked, give the single number slowly or switch to English — that's natural code-switching, not failure. Tier 2 below shows how a native includes them. Queenie also sharpened the noun: 贸易份额 (trade share) rather than the generic 市场份额.
07 · REFINING
我们也是全球最大的炼糖商之一，产能达到四百五十万吨左右。
Wǒmen yě shì quánqiú zuì dà de liàntángshāng zhī yī, chǎnnéng dádào sìbǎi wǔshí wàn dūn zuǒyòu.
We are also one of the world's largest sugar refiners, with capacity reaching around 4.5 million tonnes.
Why this phrasing之一 ("one of") is the precision hedge — you're the largest trader but one of the largest refiners, and Chinese audiences register when superlatives are used carefully. 4.5 million = 四百五十万 — again, memorise as a chunk.
08 · AUSTRALIA
我们是澳大利亚最大的糖厂，也生产乙醇和电力。
Wǒmen shì Àodàlìyà zuì dà de tángchǎng, yě shēngchǎn yǐchún hé diànlì.
We are Australia’s largest sugar miller, and we also produce ethanol and electricity.
Why this phrasingQueenie’s corrections: 生产 ("produce") for physical output — 也生产乙醇和电力 — and she flipped the word order to 我们是澳大利亚最大的糖厂, noting both orders work: "swap freely, just don’t change the content." She gave you the same freedom on hedges: 达到…左右 / 大概 / 大约 are interchangeable, so you’re not chained to one memorised form.
09 · INDIA
在印度，我们是最大的乙醇公司。
Zài Yìndù, wǒmen shì zuì dà de yǐchún gōngsī.
In India, we are the largest ethanol company.
10 · CONSUMER BRANDS
我们在澳新和印度也有很多知名的消费品牌。
Wǒmen zài Àoxīn hé Yìndù yě yǒu hěn duō zhīmíng de xiāofèi pǐnpái.
In Australia–New Zealand and India, we also have many well-known consumer brands.
Why this phrasing澳新 is the standard two-character compression of Australia–New Zealand — the exact parallel of "ANZ" — and closing the tour on consumer brands ends on something the audience can picture on a shelf, not a statistic.

PART 3文化背景Cultural context

From this lesson — the context behind the language

甲乙丙丁 — the Chinese A, B, C, D: the first four of the ten Heavenly Stems (天干: 甲乙丙丁戊己庚辛壬癸), a 3,000-year-old ordering system still in daily use. Three places you'll meet it: chemistry — alcohols are named by position, so 甲醇 is methanol ("A-alcohol", the industrial one that kills in fake liquor) and 乙醇 is ethanol ("B-alcohol" — yours: drinkable, fermentable, fuel); contracts — 甲方 / 乙方 is Party A / Party B in every Chinese agreement you will ever sign, with 甲方 the commissioning or power side; and the calendar — Stems paired with the twelve Earthly Branches create the 60-year cycle, which is why a 60th birthday (六十大寿) carries a weight in Chinese culture that no Western milestone matches.
Spelling out your name: nobody retains a Chinese given name on first hearing, so the custom is to pin each character with a phrase everyone knows: 我姓郭 — 祖先的祖，扬名海外的扬. Queenie does it herself (智慧的慧，茉莉花的莉). From you, 扬名海外 ("famous overseas") lands with a self-aware touch of humour — and it makes the name stick.
The humility ritual: 多多包涵 means "please be forgiving" — deliberately chosen over 多多指教 ("please instruct me"). After your disclaimer, expect the ritual compliment 「你的中文很好！」; the correct move is a smile and 哪里哪里，我还在学习. From a senior foreigner, self-deprecation is charm, not weakness — this exchange wins more goodwill than fluency would.
Talking about Taiwan — the red line: never 国家 (country); say 台湾 or, if a category is needed, 地区 (region). On the mainland, plain 台湾 is safest — and the same care applies in Taiwan itself, where society splits blue (KMT, 中华民国 framing) and green (DPP, independence-leaning). The mutual-understanding foundation dates to the 1993 Koo–Wang talks (辜汪会谈), hosted in Singapore by Lee Kuan Yew — and Singapore's own position recognises one China, so as a Singaporean executive, studied neutrality is your correct register on both sides of the strait.
中资 · 外资 · 合资 — who owns the company: the mainland habitually classifies firms by capital origin: 中资 Chinese-owned, 外资 foreign-owned, 合资 joint venture (中外合资), plus 港资 / 台资 / 澳资 for Hong Kong, Taiwan and Macau capital — which are never called 外资; that's politically sensitive. Separately: 国企 = state-owned enterprise, 央企 = the top tier administered directly by the centre (e.g. 中铁). Expect the question 「你们是中资还是外资？」 about 益海嘉里 (Yihai Kerry — the 金龙鱼 brand, Shenzhen-listed with Singapore parentage) and have your one-line answer ready.
糖王 — the Sugar King: Robert Kuok's mainland nickname. Queenie recounted living through China's sugar shortage — imports of raw Cuban sugar, dates from Iraq for sweetness — and how Kuok's sourcing for China earned him real standing there. Given the Kuok family connection to Wilmar, older Chinese counterparts may raise this history with you: it is relationship capital, know it well. Bonus vocabulary from the same exchange: 糖蜜 (tángmì, molasses — your ethanol feedstock; not 蜜糖, which is honey) and 扬名海外 doing double duty in your name.

PART 5练习Practice exercises

Interactive practice

How this is built: each mode applies a finding from learning science. Retrieval practice (testing yourself beats re-reading — Roediger & Karpicke), desirable difficulty (Bjork — effortful recall with fading cues builds durable memory), the production effect (saying it aloud encodes better than silent reading), and within-session spacing (missed items recycle back later, not immediately). Do one full pass daily for 4–5 days rather than one long session — spacing beats massing.
Retrieval practice — the sentence appears with a key word missing. Recall it before looking at the options, say it aloud, then tap to check. Missed items come back at the end of the round.
Syntax chunking — rebuild each sentence by tapping the word chips in order. This trains word order as chunks, not isolated words — the difference between reciting and speaking. Tap a placed chip to remove it.
Production + spacing — English prompt: say the Chinese aloud from memory, then tap the card to check. Grade yourself honestly: "Again" recycles the card into this round (Leitner-style); "Got it" retires it. The round ends when everything is retired.
Tap to reveal
Phonological accuracy — pick the correct pinyin (with tones) for each key word. Tones are where dormant O-Level Chinese decays first, and where errors are most audible to a native ear. Say the word aloud with the tone contour after each answer.
Fading cues — deliver the whole speech aloud at each rung, with less support every time. When you can do rung 4 smoothly twice, you're performance-ready. This is scaffolded retrieval: each rung is a desirable difficulty, not a memory test.

Delivery notes

🔊 Audio: the speaker buttons use your iPhone's built-in Mandarin voice (Tingting). If audio ever stops working, a full phone restart usually resets the speech engine.
The one tone that matters: 糖 (táng, sugar) is second tone — rising. First tone (tāng) is 汤 — soup. Get this wrong and you've introduced yourself as the Group Head of Soup.
丰益 — bite the yì: Queenie had you exaggerate the second syllable (益, falling tone) because a lazy yì blurs into 丰裕. Over-articulate in practice; it settles to normal in performance.
Your systematic weak spot — y-/i- initials: Queenie observed you swallow them (业务 yèwù, 乙醇 yǐchún, 已经 yǐjīng). When drilling, lean into every y- syllable deliberately.
Slow is senior. Deliberate pacing with pauses at the commas reads as gravitas, not weakness — and buys you thinking time.
Numbers: 十六年 (shíliù nián, 16 years) and 十年 (shí nián, 10 years). Practise these aloud — numbers under pressure are where learners stumble.
After the intro: expect 「你的中文很好！」. Smile, say 哪里哪里，我还在学习 (nǎli nǎli, wǒ hái zài xuéxí). This exchange, done well, wins more goodwill than the speech itself.
